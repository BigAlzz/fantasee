# "Finn" Narrative Style Guide
## Isekai Medical Drama — Third-Person Literary Narration

Derived from the YouTube transcript of a ~2h40m isekai story about a modern ICU physician reborn in a medieval peasant boy's body. This guide captures the exact narrative DNA so an LLM can generate stories with the same voice, rhythm, and emotional architecture.

---

## 1. SENTENCE STRUCTURE — Staccato Realism

**Short, declarative sentences dominate.** Average sentence length is 8–15 words. Fragments are used deliberately for rhythm, emphasis, and pacing — never as errors.

**Pattern: catalogue + image**
```
His name is Finn. He's 13. His father died last winter.
His mother's name is Isa. His sister is Macy. She's six.
She's lying in the corner on straw wheezing.
```

**Pattern: action chain (no conjunctions)**
```
She puts the knife on the shelf. She grabs a pot. She goes outside.
```

**Pattern: fragment for emotional punch**
```
Not a stranger, his sister.
```

**Pattern: two-word or three-word sentences for dramatic weight**
```
It works.
She nods.
He waited.
```

**RULES:**
- Use periods, not commas, to separate sequential observations.
- Fragments are welcome when they land like a hammer: "Not from excitement, from understanding."
- Never write a sentence longer than 25 words unless it's a deliberate "breath" passage (reflection, cataloguing knowledge, internal monologue).
- Conjunctions ("and", "but", "so") are used sparingly — mostly at the start of sentences for contrast, never to string clauses into run-ons.

---

## 2. NARRATIVE VOICE — Clinical Intimacy

The narrator is **third-person omniscient but glued to the protagonist's skin**. The camera is inside the character's head, seeing what they see, knowing what they know — but narrated in third person, not first.

The voice carries two registers simultaneously:
- **Medical-clinical precision** — diagnoses, procedures, and observations rendered with textbook accuracy.
- **Literary emotional depth** — feelings described through the body, never named abstractly.

**Example of dual register:**
```
Her skin is dry and hot, like a heated stone. Pink spots on her stomach,
a cracked tongue. Her breathing is rapid and shallow. Typhus. The ninth
or tenth day. A fever over 40. Without help, she'll die by morning.
Maybe sooner.
```
The first sentence is sensory. The second is diagnostic. The third is clinical shorthand. The fourth is a death sentence delivered flatly. This alternation IS the voice.

**RULES:**
- Medical terms are used untranslated — the audience catches the weight from context, not explanation.
- Internal thoughts are rendered as indirect discourse, never italicized or tagged: "He knew this as surely as he knew his own name. Both names."
- The narrator never editorializes ("sadly", "fortunately"). The facts carry the emotion.

---

## 3. DIALOGUE — Sparse, Functional, Unattributed

Dialogue is **minimal and direct**. Characters speak in short, practical sentences. There are almost no dialogue tags ("he said angrily"). Attribution is handled through action beats.

**Pattern: dialogue sandwiched in action**
```
Finn stands up. His legs are shaking, but he stands up. He tells his
mother to put all the water in the house on the stove, every pot,
every kettle.
```
(Indirect dialogue — no quotes, no "he said". This is the DEFAULT mode.)

**Pattern: direct dialogue with action beat, no tag**
```
She asks for bread. Ayla presses her hands to her mouth. She doesn't
make a sound.
```

**Pattern: dialogue that reveals character through word choice**
```
He said he wasn't treating the enemy. He was treating a human being.
```

**RULES:**
- Indirect dialogue is preferred over direct. "He told her to boil water" > "He said, 'Boil the water.'"
- Direct dialogue (in quotes) is reserved for moments of high emotional stakes, confrontation, or when exact wording matters.
- Dialogue tags are almost never used. If the speaker needs identification, use an action beat: "Finn wipes the sweat from his sister's forehead and tells his mother to make broth."
- Characters never explain themselves unless cornered. They state facts, give orders, or ask questions.
- Children's dialogue is simple and naive, often accidentally profound: "eyes are heavier than mouths and harder to lift."

---

## 4. SENSORY DETAIL — Concrete, Visceral, Grounded

The world is described through **physical sensation**, not adjective strings. Every description has a texture, a smell, a temperature.

**Smell is the primary sense:**
```
The darkness smells of smoke and urine.
The air is so thick you could chew it.
```

**Touch is the second:**
```
The cold stings his feet so badly it takes his breath away.
Her skin is dry and hot, like a heated stone.
```

**Sound is always specific:**
```
A wet, gurgling wheeze from the corner.
The snow crunches under his feet.
```

**RULES:**
- One dominant sense per scene, one supporting sense. Don't stack all five.
- Similes use common, physical objects: "like a heated stone", "like a wet rope", "like those of a newborn foal."
- No abstract atmosphere ("a melancholy gloom"). Always physical: "a dim fire in the hearth."
- The world is ugly, dirty, cold, and real. No romanticizing poverty.

---

## 5. EMOTIONAL ARCHITECTURE — Show the Body, Not the Feeling

**Emotions are NEVER named.** They are shown through:
- Physical sensations (hands trembling, teeth gritting, throat tightening)
- Actions (pressing hands to mouth, gripping the knife, walking out in silence)
- What characters DON'T do (She isn't praying. She doesn't wipe the tears.)

**Pattern: action as emotional shorthand**
```
Isa sits against the wall, motionless. Only her hands clench on her knees.
She isn't praying. Finn notices this, so she no longer believes.
```
(No "she was devastated." The clenched hands and the absence of prayer say it all.)

**Pattern: physical detail as internal state**
```
He looks at his hands. Small, dirty, calluses on his fingers from the
bow string. This boy used to shoot a bow.
```

**RULES:**
- Zero emotional adjectives: never "sadly", "angrily", "fearfully", "hopefully."
- Zero "he felt" or "she thought" constructions. Use "he knew", "he understood", "he noticed" — these are perception, not emotion.
- Tears are shown by their effects (streaming down cheeks, not being wiped away), never by "she cried."
- Silence and absence are as powerful as action. What a character doesn't do carries equal weight.

---

## 6. KNOWLEDGE TRANSFER — The Isekai Engine

The protagonist's modern knowledge is the story's engine. It's revealed through **diagnostic moments** — the character sees something, recognizes the condition, and acts.

**Pattern: observation → diagnosis → action**
```
The wound was below the knee. It was a gash. It was dirty. The edges
were red and swollen. There was pus. The smell was sweet and heavy.
The wound was about 5 days old, maybe older. No one had treated it.
No one had stitched it up. They just wrapped it in a rag and left it.
[...] Lymphangitis. The infection was spreading through the lymphatic
vessels. Two more days and sepsis would set in. Three more and the
boy would die.
```

**RULES:**
- Medical knowledge is delivered as the character's internal monologue — not as exposition to the reader.
- Technical terms are used without explanation. Context makes them intelligible.
- The contrast between modern knowledge and medieval ignorance is NEVER stated explicitly. It's shown through the character's frustration, the villagers' bewilderment, and the gap between what the protagonist knows and what he can do.
- Each knowledge application is a complete micro-arc: recognition → plan → execution → result.

---

## 7. PACING — The Rhythm of Crisis and Quiet

**Crisis mode:** Rapid-fire short sentences. One per line. Staccato rhythm. Present-tense urgency.
```
He washes the wound with boiled water. Tom screams. Finn holds him
by the ankle and doesn't stop. He squeezes out the pus. He rinses
it again. He applies honey, a thick layer.
```

**Quiet mode:** Longer sentences, cataloguing observations, reflective. Past and present blur.
```
He was a pediatrician and an intensive care physician. He worked
36-hour shifts. He lost patients. He drank coffee from paper cups.
And now he's sitting on the dirt floor holding the hand of a
six-year-old girl who isn't his.
```

**RULES:**
- Alternate between crisis and quiet. Never sustain one mode for more than 400 words.
- After every major scene, there's a quiet beat — the character alone, reflecting, usually looking at their hands or at the sky.
- Time skips are handled with single transitional sentences: "For 3 days, Finn didn't leave Macy's side." No elaborate time-passing montages.
- Lists and catalogues are used for pacing: counting patients, naming herbs, listing supplies. They create rhythm and convey competence.

---

## 8. CHARACTER INTRODUCTION — Action First, Name Later

Characters are introduced through **what they're doing**, not who they are.

**Pattern:**
```
A woman stands in the doorway. Thin dark circles under her eyes, a
knife in her hand, not a kitchen knife, the kind used to slaughter
livestock. Aya, mother.
```
(We see her before we know her name or relationship.)

**RULES:**
- Physical description is always one or two concrete details, never a full-body catalogue.
- A character's role or relationship is revealed through context, not exposition.
- Supporting characters are defined by ONE distinguishing trait or action: Sadie = fierce eyes + carries coal. Mea = skinny + stick + venom. Haldor = twirling his cap.
- Names are given after the character has been physically established.

---

## 9. THEMATIC PATTERNS

Recurring motifs that should appear in every story:

- **Hands** — the protagonist constantly looks at his hands. They are the bridge between worlds. They heal, they shoot, they hold.
- **Clean vs. dirty** — boiled water vs. filthy rags, clean house vs. sooty hut. Hygiene is ideology.
- **Silence as communication** — characters who don't speak say the most. Nods, looks, turns, walking away.
- **Children as moral compass** — Macy, Tom, the unnamed dying boy. Children are the stakes. Every adult decision is measured against its effect on children.
- **The cost of knowledge** — knowing how to save someone and being unable to is worse than not knowing at all.

---

## 10. WHAT TO AVOID

- ❌ Exposition dumps explaining the world's magic system or politics
- ❌ Dialogue tags: "he said", "she exclaimed", "he whispered"
- ❌ Emotional adjectives: "sadly", "angrily", "fearfully"
- ❌ Romantic subplots as primary drivers (relationships exist, but serve the mission)
- ❌ Power fantasy / OP protagonist — the character fails, loses patients, gets exhausted
- ❌ Long action sequences — combat is brief, tactical, and described from the commander's perspective, not the warrior's
- ❌ Internal monologue in first person (even though the character has memories of a past life)
- ❌ Purple prose or lyrical descriptions — the beauty is in the precision, not the ornament
- ❌ Characters who explain their motivations — their actions speak
- ❌ "Suddenly" or "unexpectedly" — things just happen, narrated flatly

## 11. EXAMPLE PASSAGE (demonstrating all rules)

```
The door creaks. A woman stands in the doorway. Thin dark circles
under her eyes, a knife in her hand, not a kitchen knife, the kind
used to slaughter livestock.

Aya, mother.

She looks at him and he reads everything in her eyes. Tomorrow she'll
slaughter the last goat to give the money to the monk so that he'll
pray over Macy. She doesn't know any other ways to treat her.

Finn stands up. His legs are shaking, but he stands up.

He tells his mother to put all the water in the house on the stove,
every pot, every kettle.

Aya looks at him as if he were speaking a foreign language.

He asks her to trust him just this once. Just once. If their daughter
isn't better by morning, she can do whatever she wants. But right now,
he needs boiling water. A lot of it.

Aya doesn't move. She grips the knife.

Then she looks at her wheezing daughter, who already reeks of death.

She puts the knife on the shelf. She grabs a pot. She goes outside.
```

**This passage demonstrates:**
- Staccato sentence structure ✓
- Character through action (gripping knife, then putting it down) ✓
- No emotional adjectives ✓
- Indirect dialogue preferred ✓
- Sensory detail (smell: "reeks of death") ✓
- Silence and gesture as communication ✓
- No dialogue tags ✓
- The mother's entire emotional arc in three physical actions ✓
